# Random Stimulus

Force a connection between your problem and something completely unrelated: a random word, object, or image. From Edward de Bono's lateral thinking work. The brain is a pattern-matching engine that will always try to bridge the gap, and the search pulls your thinking into territory it would not explore otherwise.

**1. State the problem clearly.** Write it as a single sentence. You will keep returning to it.

- _"How do we reduce onboarding friction for enterprise customers averaging 14 days to first value?"_

**2. Generate a genuinely random stimulus.** Random word generator, dictionary page, random object from the room, or stock photo.

- The stimulus must actually be random. Do not search for a word that "fits." That defeats the method.
- Nouns work best. Concrete beats abstract. _"Lighthouse"_ beats _"efficiency."_

**3. List the stimulus's properties.** Unpack the stimulus before connecting it to your problem. Features, functions, contexts, sensory qualities.

- For _"lighthouse"_: tall, solitary, warning, guiding, rhythmic flash, coastal, automated, visible from far away, stands against storms.
- Aim for 8-15 associations. More raw material means more possible connections.

**4. Force connections to the problem.** Go through each association and ask: "How could this apply?" Write down every connection, even absurd ones.

- _"Visible from far away"_ – a progress indicator showing how close the customer is to first value?
- _"Rhythmic flash"_ – short daily nudges instead of one long setup flow?
- _"Stands against storms"_ – how resilient is onboarding when a customer hits their first support issue?

**5. Harvest real ideas.** Review connections. Some are absurd, a few are genuinely useful. The value is rarely the first connection. Usually the third or fourth, after your brain stops trying to be sensible.

**6. Try a second stimulus if needed.** Different stimuli surface different angles. Two or three rounds is usually enough.

- Works well with a partner or small group. A word useless to one person might spark a breakthrough for another.

**Practical notes:** The silliness you feel is a feature, not a bug. It bypasses your usual patterns. Keep a list of random prompts in your notebook for when projects stall. Pairs well mid-session when a classic brainstorm runs dry. However, two or three rounds per problem is the practical ceiling before diminishing returns.
