# Cost-Benefit (Expected Value)

Cost-benefit analysis tallies costs and benefits to see if an option is worth pursuing. Expected value extends it by weighting each outcome by its probability. Both force you to put numbers on things and make tradeoffs explicit. Done well, they clarify decisions. Done sloppily, they produce false confidence.

**1. Define the decision and time horizon.** Numbers only make sense within a scope.
- _"Should we build the MSP partner portal in-house or license a white-label product, evaluated over three years?"_
- A fixed horizon makes costs and benefits comparable. Open-ended horizons let the spreadsheet drift toward any conclusion.

**2. Enumerate the costs.** All of them: direct, indirect, one-time, recurring, and opportunity.
- **Direct:** license fees, headcount, infrastructure.
- **Indirect:** onboarding time, integration effort, maintenance burden.
- **Opportunity:** what else could this money or team accomplish? A $400K build means $400K not spent on demand generation.

**3. Enumerate the benefits.** Same discipline. Do not just list the obvious ones.
- Revenue (new or retained), cost savings, time savings, risk reduction, strategic optionality.
- Quantify what you can. For benefits that resist quantification (brand perception, team morale), note them separately. A fabricated $200K "brand value" undermines the entire model.

**4. Add probabilities where outcomes are uncertain.** This is where expected value enters.
- _"30% chance of winning the Tier-1 enterprise deal at $4M ARR"_ = expected value of $1.2M.
- Do not pretend to three decimal places. Ranges (20-40%) and midpoints are more honest than single-number guesses.

**5. Compute expected net value.** Sum probability-weighted benefits, subtract probability-weighted costs.
- Positive net value means the option is worth pursuing on these assumptions.
- Compare multiple options on the same basis. If Option A nets $800K and Option B nets $750K, that $50K gap may be within your estimation error.

**6. Run sensitivity analysis.** Numbers are estimates. Vary the key assumptions and see if the decision flips.
- If the conclusion holds across reasonable ranges, you have a robust decision.
- If it depends on one optimistic assumption (a 50% conversion rate when your historical average is 28%), you have a fragile one. Reduce that uncertainty before committing.

**Practical notes:** The hardest part is being honest about low-probability, high-magnitude outcomes. A 5% chance of catastrophe can dominate expected value; a 5% chance of enormous upside can justify a borderline project. Yet, resist using expected value for decisions where the downside is genuinely unacceptable regardless of probability. Some risks (reputational damage, regulatory penalties) should not be averaged into a spreadsheet. Use this method for comparable, repeatable decisions. Use judgment for one-way doors.
