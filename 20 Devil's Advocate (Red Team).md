# Devil's Advocate (Red Team)

Devil's advocate and red teaming are institutionalized disagreement. Someone is formally assigned to attack the plan: find flaws, stress-test assumptions, argue the opposite conclusion. The point is to surface honest opposition without the social cost people normally pay for it.

**1. Make the assignment explicit.** Someone is _the_ devil's advocate, and everyone knows it. They are not being contrarian. They are doing their job.
- _"Sara is running red team on our Q3 go-to-market plan. Her job for the next hour is to find every reason this strategy is wrong."_
- The role must be visible. Implicit devil's advocacy gets read as personal disagreement and triggers defensiveness.

**2. Choose the advocate carefully.** The best advocates are respected, senior enough to push hard, and not the same person every time.
- Avoid assigning it to someone who already opposes the plan. They will produce predictable attacks the team dismisses as biased.
- Assigning the role to a likely supporter is counterintuitive but effective. No one can attribute the critique to personal agenda.

**3. Give real prep time.** Red teaming works when the opposition has actually studied the plan. Rushed advocates produce shallow objections easy to wave off.
- Share the full plan 24-48 hours in advance. For a $2M budget review, the red team needs the same data the planners used.
- For larger decisions, run the red team as a separate session, not a 15-minute segment at the end.

**4. Structure the attack.** Unstructured criticism devolves into nitpicks. Give the red team a framework: challenge assumptions, identify failure modes, steelman the alternative, find counter-evidence.
- _"Our plan assumes 35% MQL-to-SQL conversion. What if it is 20%? What changes?"_
- Write objections down with the underlying assumption each one targets.

**5. Respond substantively, not defensively.** The presenting team must engage seriously: accept, counter with evidence, or park as a known risk.
- _"Fair concern. Here is why we are accepting that risk, and here is our trigger to revisit"_ is a valid answer.
- It may seem that dismissing objections quickly keeps the meeting moving. Yet, it signals the exercise is performative, and the next advocate will not bother preparing.

**6. Decide after the attack, not during.** The advocate's job is to generate quality opposition, not to co-own the decision.
- After the session, decision-makers weigh the objections and decide.
- Thank the advocate publicly. Reward good red teaming visibly, or the role becomes career poison.

**Practical notes:** The technique fails when one person is cast as the permanent skeptic. That flattens everyone else into passive agreement. Rotate the role aggressively. It also fails when objections can be overruled by seniority without explanation. For high-stakes decisions (major investments, product launches with lock-in), consider a full red team of two to four people with a full day, not just an assigned role in a 60-minute meeting.
