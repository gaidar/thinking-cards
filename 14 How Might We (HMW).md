# How Might We (HMW)

A reframing technique from IDEO that converts problems into generative questions. The phrasing does real work: _how_ assumes solutions exist; _might_ keeps options open; _we_ makes it collaborative. The right HMW sits in a sweet spot: narrow enough to focus, broad enough for diverse answers. Finding that sweet spot is the entire skill.

**1. Start with the raw problem or insight.** A research finding, a customer complaint, a metric moving wrong, a strategic gap.
- _"MSP partners drop off during onboarding because they cannot tell what steps remain."_
- Concrete observations produce better HMW questions than abstract ones.

**2. Write the first HMW attempt.** Convert the problem into a _"How might we..."_ question.
- _"How might we make onboarding requirements visible to partners at every stage?"_
- Do not embed a solution. _"How might we add a progress bar?"_ is a solution wearing a question's clothes.

**3. Test the scope (goldilocks check).** Neither too narrow nor too broad.
- **Too narrow:** _"How might we add tooltips to step three?"_ – only one answer fits.
- **Too broad:** _"How might we improve the partner experience?"_ – any answer fits.
- **Right scope:** _"How might we help partners know exactly what is required and how far along they are?"_

**4. Generate variations.** The same problem produces multiple framings, each opening a different solution space. Write three to five.
- _"How might we make the requirements impossible to miss?"_
- _"How might we remove the requirements entirely?"_
- _"How might we have the system complete steps automatically?"_
- Elimination, automation, visibility, self-service: same problem, different territories.

**5. Pick the most generative framing.** Select the variant that opens the most interesting space while pointing at the real problem.
- The one that makes the room lean in and start suggesting ideas is usually right.
- Park the others for when the first framing runs dry.

**6. Use it as the prompt for ideation.** The chosen HMW becomes the header for brainstorming, brainwriting, or Crazy 8s. Every idea should answer that question.
- If ideation stalls, check the framing first. A stalled session usually means the question is too narrow, too broad, or hides an assumption.

**Practical notes:** HMW bridges _define_ and _ideate_ in design thinking. Good questions emerge from pain points, surprising research, or "what if the opposite were true" inversions. Spend the extra five minutes generating variations. The first framing that sounds reasonable is rarely the best. If your HMW can be answered with one specific feature you already had in mind, rewrite it.
