# Dot Voting

Dot voting is a quick group prioritization method. Everyone gets a fixed number of votes (sticky dots, markers, or digital stars) and distributes them across options. The constraint is the point: scarcity forces people to reveal what they actually care most about rather than politely agreeing with everything.

**1. Prepare the options.** Options must be visible, comparable, and at similar abstraction levels.
- Mixing _"rebuild the entire partner portal"_ with _"fix a typo on the pricing page"_ skews results.
- Group near-duplicates first so votes are not split across the same idea.
- _"Before we vote, merge the three overlapping lead scoring ideas into one option."_

**2. Decide the voting formula.** Standard rule: **N/3 votes** per person, where N is the number of options. Ten options means three votes.
- Fewer votes produces sharper signal. More votes dilutes it; everyone votes for everything.
- Let people stack votes however they want: all three on one option or spread across three.
- For strict ranking needing a single winner, restrict to one vote per option.

**3. Explain the criterion.** Voting means nothing without a shared question. Different criteria produce different winners.
- _"Which campaign ideas should we prioritize for Q3 pipeline generation?"_
- _"Which product gaps are most likely to cause churn in the next 90 days?"_
- Write the criterion on the board where everyone can see it.

**4. Vote privately or publicly – choose deliberately.** Public voting creates bandwagon effects. Private voting produces more honest signal.
- For genuine prioritization, use private voting: simultaneous placement on a countdown.
- _"Place your dots on the count of three."_
- Public voting is fine for lightweight decisions where social alignment is desirable.

**5. Reveal, discuss, and validate.** Count dots. Look for patterns beyond the winner.
- Clear winner with significant margin: act on it.
- Cluster of near-ties: dot voting has diagnosed a disagreement. Have a real conversation.
- _"Three options have four votes each. What is the actual trade-off we need to resolve?"_
- Options with zero votes can be dropped entirely.

**6. Use the result as input, not verdict.** Dot voting surfaces group preference. It does not override strategy, expertise, or authority.
- If the team votes for a feature conflicting with a strategic MSP commitment, that is a conversation, not a mandate.
- _"The vote tells us where the team's energy is. The decision still accounts for Q3 targets and existing commitments."_

**Practical notes:** Dot voting's two failures: giving too many votes (no signal) and letting the result substitute for judgment (counting replaces thinking). The N/3 rule is a reliable default. Silent and simultaneous is the safest variant. Yet, the real value is speed: it turns 20 messy items into a shortlist of 3 to 5 in under five minutes. Miro, Mural, and FigJam have it built in.
