# Brainwriting (6-3-5)

Six people, three ideas, five minutes per round. Brainstorming's quieter, more egalitarian cousin. It solves the two biggest problems of verbal brainstorming: loud voices dominate, and hearing others' ideas too early narrows your own thinking. Produces roughly 40% more ideas than verbal brainstorming in the same time.

**1. Set up the sheets.** Each participant gets a sheet divided into three columns and six rows. Write the problem statement at the top of every sheet.

- Paper or digital both work, though paper feels faster.

**2. Frame the prompt.** Sharp, specific, generative. Too broad scatters the output; too narrow yields variations of one idea.

- _"How might we shorten MSP partner onboarding from 14 days to 5?"_
- _"What could we change about the trial-to-paid conversion flow?"_

**3. Round one: three ideas, five minutes.** Everyone writes three ideas silently in their top row. No talking.

- Ideas can be short: a sentence or rough sketch. The time constraint forces commitment over perfection.

**4. Pass and build.** Pass sheets to the next person. They read existing ideas and add three new ones: extending, combining, inverting, or going sideways.

- Silence is maintained throughout. Building happens on paper, not out loud.

**5. Repeat until full.** Six rounds, 30 minutes, 18 ideas per sheet, 108 total. Energy fades around round four. Push through. Later rounds produce the most unusual ideas because obvious ones are taken.

**6. Cluster, discuss, converge.** Break the silence. Lay out all sheets, cluster similar ideas, and converge using dot voting or an impact-effort matrix.

- By this point, ideas are on the table instead of trapped in the loudest person's head. Discussion is about selection, not generation.

**Practical notes:** Especially useful for mixed groups: senior and junior, introverts and extroverts, native and non-native speakers. The format neutralizes hierarchy because no one knows whose idea is whose until discussion. The 6-3-5 numbers are not sacred; four people and four minutes works fine. The key principles: silent, parallel, rotating.
