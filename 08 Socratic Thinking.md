# Socratic Thinking

Socratic questioning is a disciplined sequence of question types, not a free-for-all debate. Use it in strategy meetings, 1:1s, or solo to stress-test reasoning before committing to a position.

**1. Clarify the claim.** Pin down what is actually being said. Most meeting debates are two people disagreeing about different things.

- _"What exactly do you mean when you say our MSP partners are underperforming?"_
- _"Can you give me a concrete example from last quarter?"_

**2. Surface the assumptions.** Every claim rests on things taken for granted. "We need to double ad spend" assumes current campaigns work and just need volume. Expose assumptions before evaluating the claim.

- _"What are we assuming about our current conversion rates?"_
- _"What would have to be true for this pricing change to work?"_

**3. Probe the reasoning.** Test the logic connecting claims to evidence. Weak arguments crumble here.

- _"What data supports the belief that SaaS retention will improve with this feature?"_
- _"How reliable is a customer survey with a 12% response rate?"_

**4. Introduce alternative viewpoints.** Steelman the opposite case: genuine perspective-taking, not theatrical devil's advocacy.

- _"What is the strongest case against expanding into this vertical?"_
- _"Who benefits from the opposite conclusion, and what do they know that we don't?"_

**5. Trace the implications.** Follow the claim to its consequences. Flawed ideas reveal themselves here because people rarely think two moves ahead.

- _"If we commit to this partner program redesign, what does that mean for Q3 headcount?"_
- _"Where does this logic break down at 10x our current scale?"_

**6. Meta-question the question.** Check whether you are even asking the right thing. Teams routinely spend hours optimizing answers to questions that should never have been asked.

- _"Is low NPS the real problem, or a symptom of something in onboarding?"_
- _"What are we not asking that we should be?"_

**Practical notes:** Ask one question at a time and wait for the answer. Silence is where thinking happens. Keep your tone curious, not prosecutorial. Short on time? Run just steps 2 and 5 to catch most weak arguments in under ten minutes.
