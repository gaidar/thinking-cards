# Problem Reversal

Problem reversal (also called inversion) flips the question. Instead of "how do we achieve X," ask "how would we guarantee the opposite?" Then invert the answers. Charlie Munger built much of his investment philosophy on Jacobi's principle: _"Invert, always invert."_ It works because avoiding stupidity is often easier than achieving brilliance.

**1. State the goal clearly.** One sentence, straightforward positive claim.
- _"Increase partner retention from 78% to 90%."_
- _"Hit 500 signups in the first week of our Q3 launch."_

**2. Invert the goal.** Rewrite as its opposite. Take the inversion seriously.
- _"How would we destroy partner retention and drive it below 50%?"_
- _"How would we guarantee zero signups?"_
- It should feel uncomfortable to write. That discomfort signals genuine inversion.

**3. Brainstorm the inverted answers.** List every concrete way to guarantee failure. Be thorough.
- _"Require partners to call and wait 20 minutes to renew."_
- _"Change pricing without notice two weeks before launch."_
- _"Hide the signup page behind three clicks and a login wall."_
- Do not filter. The goal is a complete map of failure modes.

**4. Look for answers uncomfortably close to what you already do.** This is the payoff. Many "how to guarantee failure" items will resemble your current process, just less extreme.
- If _"require a phone call to renew"_ made the list and your renewal involves a 15-minute scheduling call, you have found something.
- The uncomfortable ones are the most valuable: failure modes you are already partially executing.

**5. Invert each failure mode into a concrete action.** Especially the uncomfortably close ones.
- _"Phone call to renew"_ → _"Single-click renewal in the partner portal."_
- _"Pricing changes without notice"_ → _"Lock launch pricing 60 days out, communicate in writing."_
- _"80-page onboarding doc"_ → _"2-page quick-start card with links to deeper docs."_

**6. Prioritize by avoidance value.** Rank inverted actions by severity of the failure, its likelihood, and ease of the fix.
- Focus first on high-probability, high-impact failure modes you are already close to.
- File low-probability ones as watch items for quarterly review.

**Practical notes:** Problem reversal is especially powerful for launches and pricing decisions, situations where failure cost is high and asymmetric. Run it solo first, then with the group. Solo inversion surfaces failures you privately suspect but would not raise in a meeting. Group inversion surfaces failures in someone else's domain. Combining both produces the most honest list.
