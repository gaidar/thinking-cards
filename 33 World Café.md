# World Café

World Café hosts genuine conversation in large groups (12 to 200+ people) by simulating café intimacy. Small groups of four to five sit at tables, discuss a question for 20 to 30 minutes, then reshuffle for the next round. Developed by Juanita Brown and David Isaacs in 1995, it works because small-group conversation produces real dialogue and rotation cross-pollinates ideas across the entire room.

**1. Design the questions.** Quality depends almost entirely on this. You need three to five questions that build on each other; each round deepens the previous one.
- _"What is the biggest obstacle to trust between our sales team and our MSP partners?"_ beats _"How can we improve the partner program?"_ The first provokes honesty; the second invites platitudes.
- _"If we could only fix one thing about customer onboarding, what would create the most retention impact?"_
- Each question should feel worth 20 minutes. If your team could answer it in two minutes, it is too narrow.

**2. Set up the space.** Round tables, four to five seats each. Large paper covering each table, plus markers. Participants write and sketch throughout.
- The paper tablecloth is load-bearing: captures ideas, connects thoughts visually, leaves a record for the next group.
- _"Set the room up like a café, not a conference room. Background music, informal seating, actual coffee."_

**3. Run the first round.** Introduce the first question. Each table discusses for 20 to 30 minutes. One person volunteers as **table host**; they do not rotate.
- The host keeps conversation flowing and ensures quieter voices get space.
- _"Table hosts: make sure everyone speaks, not just the loudest."_

**4. Rotate for round two.** Everyone except the host moves to a new table with people they have not yet talked to.
- The host delivers a two-minute summary of the previous discussion.
- _"Here is what the last group wrestled with: these three tensions on the tablecloth. Now, the next question."_

**5. Continue the rotations.** Three to four rounds total. Each round uses a new question or deepens the previous one. Table paper accumulates ideas from every group.
- By round three, cross-pollination is dense. Insights recombine in ways no single conversation could produce.
- Energy peaks at round two or three. Do not cut it short.

**6. Harvest the collective insight.** Bring the whole room together. What patterns emerged? What surprised people? What questions are louder now?
- Table hosts report on the evolution of thinking across rounds, not just final conclusions.
- Photograph the table papers: raw material for affinity mapping or strategy sessions.
- _"We are extracting themes here, not making decisions. Decisions belong to a follow-up session."_

**Practical notes:** World Café works for strategy offsites, partner summits, cross-functional alignment, post-acquisition workshops. It fails when questions are too narrow or rounds are compressed below 20 minutes. Rushing produces superficial output not worth the setup. Yet, with proper time, a four-round session fills about three hours and produces more durable alignment than any format of comparable length. Pairs naturally with affinity mapping as follow-up.
