# Future Press Release

Amazon's method for defining a project before committing resources. The team writes the press release that would go out on launch day, entirely from the customer's perspective. If it reads boring, the project is not worth doing. The method forces outside-in thinking: start from what the customer will experience, then work backward.

**1. Pick a launch date.** Set a concrete date, typically twelve to twenty-four months out. Every sentence after this is written as if that date has arrived.

**2. Write the headline.** One sentence. Customer-facing. No jargon.
- _"Acronis ONE launches unified AI-driven cyber protection for MSPs, cutting incident response time from hours to minutes."_
- If the headline does not make the customer care, rewrite until it does.

**3. Write the opening paragraph.** Three to five sentences: what the product is, who it serves, why it changes their situation.
- Name the customer explicitly. Focus on outcome, not features. A press release that reads like a spec sheet has failed.

**4. Add a customer quote.** A specific, plausible quote from a named customer explaining the before-and-after.
- _"Before Acronis ONE, our team spent half their week triaging alerts across four consoles. Now we resolve incidents in the time it used to take just to identify them," said Mira Sato, CISO at NexGen IT Solutions._
- If you cannot write a plausible customer quote, you do not understand the customer well enough to build the product.

**5. List key benefits.** Three to five bullet points, benefit-focused and measurable.
- _"Incident response time reduced by 80%."_
- _"Unified console across backup, EDR, and patch management. No tool-switching."_
- _"Onboarding a new MSP technician drops from two weeks to three days."_

**6. Answer the FAQ.** Cover both external questions (customers) and internal questions (your team). This is where the hard parts surface.
- External: _"How is this different from today? What does it cost?"_
- Internal: _"What is the riskiest assumption? What is the biggest engineering dependency?"_
- If the internal answers are hand-waves, the project is not ready.

**Practical notes:** Most powerful at project kickoff: the artifact that defines the initiative before anyone writes code. A good one runs one to two pages; longer means unfocused scope. It may seem that revisiting after kickoff adds little. Yet, checking at milestones is equally important: does the thing being built still deliver the headline? Pair with Jobs to Be Done for sharper customer framing.
