# Walt Disney Method

The Walt Disney Method (formalized by Robert Dilts) separates three modes of thinking that normally collide and cancel each other out: **the Dreamer, the Realist, and the Critic**. By inhabiting each role in sequence, you give imagination, planning, and skepticism their own space. Without that separation, the Critic kills ideas before they are fully formed, and the team converges on safe incremental options every time.

**1. Set up three spaces.** Physically or mentally, designate three distinct locations for the three roles. Disney reportedly used different rooms. Most teams use different corners of a whiteboard.
- The separation matters. Mixing roles collapses them back into ordinary debate.
- Solo variant: three sheets of paper or three tabs. The physical act of switching forces a mental shift.

**2. Dreamer: what would be amazing?** Nothing is impossible. No budgets, no timelines, no legacy constraints.
- _"If we could do anything for our MSP partner program (unlimited budget, no constraints) what would we build?"_
- Think visually, concretely, ambitiously. Describe the ideal outcome in rich detail.
- The Critic is not welcome here. If objections pop up, note them on a separate page and return to dreaming.

**3. Realist: how would you actually do it?** Take the Dreamer's ideas as given and figure out execution. Constructive, pragmatic, focused on steps.
- _"What would it take? Which team? What is the sequence? How long for MVP versus full vision?"_
- Produce steps, resources, timelines, dependencies. The Realist does not judge whether the idea is worth doing, only asks _how_.

**4. Critic: what could go wrong?** Evaluate: probe for weaknesses, risks, things the Realist glossed over.
- _"What is missing? Where does it break at scale? Who inside the organization will resist, and why?"_
- The Critic is constructive, not destructive. Criticism ends with a question or a condition, not a verdict.
- _"This onboarding flow will not scale past 200 partners. Could we automate provisioning via the existing API?"_

**5. Cycle back.** The Critic's concerns return to the Dreamer (_"what is the dream that solves this?"_) then to the Realist (_"how do you build that?"_). Two or three cycles usually suffice.
- Each cycle refines the idea without killing it. The Dreamer answers critiques with imagination, not defensiveness.
- Stop when the Critic runs out of substantial objections and the Realist has a credible plan.

**6. Synthesize.** Leave the three spaces. What survived? What is the plan?
- Write a single coherent proposal carrying the Dreamer's ambition, the Realist's structure, and the Critic's risk awareness.
- The roles are session tools, not permanent identities. Once synthesized, the idea stands on its own.

**Practical notes:** The sequence matters: Dreamer _first_, Critic _last_. Reversing the order sets a ceiling the Dreamer never exceeds. For groups, rotate role assignments each round. It keeps the Critic from becoming a permanent skeptic and the Dreamer from being dismissed as unrealistic. It may seem that separating thinking modes is artificial. Yet, the separation is precisely what allows each mode to operate at full strength instead of being diluted by the others.
