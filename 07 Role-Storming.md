# Role-Storming

Brainstorming done in character. You adopt someone else's perspective (a competitor's CEO, a frustrated customer, a skeptical analyst) and ideate as they would. Most people are too close to their own problems for their best thinking. Yet, the same people freed into a role produce genuinely different output.

**1. Define the problem.** Write a specific ideation prompt visible to everyone. Every role returns to this question.
- _"How might we make our partner portal something MSPs actually want to open every morning?"_

**2. Choose roles deliberately.** Pick three to six roles representing meaningfully different angles. Combine internal and external, supportive and skeptical.
- Product ideation: the CEO of your biggest competitor, a power user managing 200 endpoints, a consumer-app designer from Spotify.
- Avoid lazy roles like "a customer." Specificity drives quality.

**3. Inhabit before ideating.** Spend one minute stepping into character. What do they care about? What would they notice first? What do they find obvious that you miss?
- A short written sketch (three sentences) helps the group share the same mental model.

**4. Ideate as the role.** Generate ideas in first person: _"If I were running Competitor X, I would kill per-endpoint pricing and go flat-rate tomorrow."_
- Commit fully. Capture each role's output separately.
- Ten to fifteen minutes per role. Less stays surface-level; more wears thin.

**5. Rotate through roles.** In groups, rotate who plays which role across rounds. A useful pattern: everyone ideates in the same role simultaneously. Shared roles produce more building on each other's ideas.

**6. Harvest and synthesize.** Lay ideas from all roles side by side. The most interesting output is often what only one specific role saw. That is where your team's assumptions were thickest.
- Combine insights across roles. The strongest final ideas often merge two perspectives that would never meet in a normal meeting.

**Practical notes:** The silliness of adopting a persona is load-bearing. It gives people permission to say things they would never say in their own voice. Specific named people, real or fictional, produce far richer output than abstract archetypes because they carry more associations. Particularly effective when your team is stuck on a problem where everyone already "knows" the answer.
