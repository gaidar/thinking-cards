# Decision Matrix (Weighted Scoring)

A decision matrix turns a judgment call into a structured comparison. List options on one axis, criteria on the other, assign weights, score, and multiply. The result is not a verdict. It is a transparent ranking that makes your reasoning visible. Works best with three to seven concrete options and a group that disagrees on priorities.

**1. Define the decision.** State what you are choosing between and why.
- _"Which marketing automation platform do we standardize on for the MSP partner program?"_
- Five to seven options is the sweet spot. Fewer makes the structure overkill; more makes it unreadable.

**2. List criteria.** Pull out the dimensions on which options differ and your team actually cares about. Five to eight criteria is usually right.
- Mix hard criteria (cost, time-to-deploy, API coverage) with soft ones (strategic fit, vendor stability).
- Avoid overlap. _"Ease of use"_ and _"user experience"_ double-count the same dimension.

**3. Assign weights.** Not all criteria are equal. Give each a weight from 1 to 5, or distribute 100 points across them.
- If everything is weighted 5, the weights are not doing any work. Force differentiation.
- Agree on weights _before_ scoring options. Otherwise people reverse-engineer weights to make their preferred vendor win.

**4. Score each option.** Use a consistent scale (1-5 or 1-10). Have each person score independently first; private scoring reduces anchoring.
- Back scores with evidence. _"Vendor A: 4 on security – SOC 2 Type II and ISO 27001 certified."_
- If data is missing, leave the cell blank. A blank is more honest than a fabricated 3.

**5. Multiply and sum.** For each option, multiply scores by weights, then sum. Highest total is the top-ranked option.
- Put the math in a spreadsheet. When your VP asks _"what if we weight integration higher?"_ you answer in ten seconds.
- The winner is the starting point for discussion, not the final answer.

**6. Sanity-check the ranking.** Does the result match collective intuition? If not, which cell drives the disconnect?
- If the math says Vendor A but your gut says Vendor B, a criterion is missing or a weight is wrong.
- It may seem that rerunning the matrix means you did it wrong. Yet, the recalculation is where the real insight lives.

**Practical notes:** The biggest risk is false precision: turning fuzzy judgments into numbers can make a close call look decisive. If the top three options are within 5% of each other, the matrix is not choosing for you. The second risk is criteria stuffing: adding criteria that favor a preferred option. Agreeing on criteria before revealing options is the main defense.
