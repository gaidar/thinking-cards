# Six Thinking Hats

Edward de Bono's Six Thinking Hats forces a group to consider the same angle at the same time. Each hat represents a mode: facts, emotions, risks, benefits, creativity, process. The whole room wears the same hat together, in sequence. Every perspective gets dedicated time, and nobody is stuck defending one role for two hours.

**1. White Hat – Facts and data.** What do you actually know? What is missing?
- Stick to verifiable information. Separate facts from interpretations.
- Note gaps explicitly. _"We do not know the renewal rate for MSP partners in APAC"_ is a useful output, not a failure.

**2. Red Hat – Emotions and intuition.** What does your gut say? No justification required.
- Each person shares feelings and hunches without defending them. 30 seconds per person.
- It may seem gut feelings have no place in a structured process. Yet, the Red Hat catches unspoken objections that would otherwise derail execution months later.

**3. Black Hat – Caution and risks.** What could go wrong? Weaknesses, obstacles, failure modes.
- The whole group wears it, so no individual gets cast as the permanent blocker.
- Be specific. _"The compliance review alone takes six weeks, and we have eight before launch"_ beats _"timeline is tight."_
- Name problems here. Solutions belong in a different hat.

**4. Yellow Hat – Optimism and benefits.** What could go right? Upside, best case, genuine value.
- Be equally rigorous about benefits as you were about risks.
- Stretch for non-obvious gains. _"If we launch this integration, it opens the door to co-selling with their entire channel."_

**5. Green Hat – Creativity and alternatives.** What else could you do? New options, wild ideas, reframes.
- _"What if we white-label the competitor's product instead of building our own?"_
- Do not evaluate here. That belongs to a different hat.

**6. Blue Hat – Process and summary.** What has the discussion produced? What is the next step?
- Usually worn by the facilitator throughout and by the whole group at the end.
- Summarize decisions, open questions, and owners. _"We have three unresolved Black Hat risks and no Green Hat alternatives for pricing. That is our next session."_
- The Blue Hat also sets the sequence. Strategic discussion: Blue, White, Green, Yellow, Black, Red, Blue. Fast review: White, Yellow, Black, Blue.

**Practical notes:** Hats are worn in sequence, not in parallel. Resist the urge to jump between them. That is exactly the chaos the method prevents. Engineers default to Black, marketing to Yellow, executives to Blue. Forcing synchronization reveals how much agreement exists once everyone looks at the same angle. A 45-minute disciplined session typically covers more ground than a two-hour freeform debate.
