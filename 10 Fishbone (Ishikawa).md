# Fishbone (Ishikawa)

The fishbone diagram maps possible causes of a problem across predefined categories. Where the 5 Whys drills one causal chain, the fishbone spreads sideways and catches multiple parallel causes. Use it when a problem is likely multi-factor: recurring incidents, quality drops, or performance collapses that defy a single explanation.

**1. Write the problem at the "head" of the fish.** Place the problem on the right in a box. Draw a horizontal spine extending left.
- _"Partner renewal rate dropped 12% last quarter"_ – specific enough.
- _"Churn issues"_ – too vague.

**2. Draw the main "bones" for cause categories.** Manufacturing uses the 6Ms (Manpower, Method, Machine, Material, Measurement, Mother Nature). For SaaS and IT: **People, Process, Technology, Tools, Environment, Data**.
- Five to seven branches. More becomes unreadable.
- MSP variant: **Staff, Workflow, Platform, Tooling, Client Environment, Metrics**.

**3. Brainstorm causes under each category.** For each branch, ask: "What in _this_ category could contribute?" Add each cause as a smaller bone.
- Invite the cross-functional group. Sales sees different causes than engineering.
- Do not filter. Get everything on the board first.

**4. Go deeper on promising causes.** Add sub-branches by asking "why." This is where fishbone and 5 Whys combine well.
- _Process → slow partner onboarding → no SLA for provisioning → ownership split between channel sales and ops._
- Two to three levels deep is usually enough. You are generating hypotheses, not proving them.

**5. Identify the most likely root causes.** Which causes appear across multiple categories? Which have the strongest evidence?
- Mark three to five as candidates for investigation.
- You are picking what to verify first, not declaring _the_ cause.

**6. Validate with data before committing to fixes.** A fishbone is a hypothesis generator, not a conclusion. Confirm top candidates against actual data before allocating budget or headcount.
- Pull metrics, talk to the people on the ground, run a small test.

**Practical notes:** The biggest failure mode is mistaking the populated diagram for the answer. A full fishbone shows _possible_ causes, not proven ones. Yet, it has a quality most tools lack: pin it to a wall, keep adding over a week, and people contribute insights they would never raise in a meeting. Teams that treat it as a living document find more root causes than those who run a single session.
