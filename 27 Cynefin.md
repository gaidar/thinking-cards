# Cynefin

Cynefin (*kuh-NEV-in*, Welsh for "habitat") is Dave Snowden's sense-making framework. Different problems demand fundamentally different responses. Most management failures stem from applying the wrong approach to the wrong kind of problem. Five domains: **Clear**, **Complicated**, **Complex**, **Chaotic**, and **Confused**.

**1. Clear.** Cause and effect are obvious. Best practices exist. **Sense, Categorize, Respond.**
- _"A new customer submits a standard onboarding request. What is the documented process?"_
- Examples: SaaS subscription renewal, Tier 1 support ticket, routine compliance audit.
- Danger: complacency. Assuming a situation is Clear when it has drifted into Complicated.

**2. Complicated.** Cause and effect are knowable but require expertise. Multiple good answers exist. **Sense, Analyze, Respond.**
- _"Our MQL-to-SQL conversion dropped 15%. What are the possible causes and which analysis do we need?"_
- Examples: architecting a multi-tenant SaaS platform, diagnosing a performance bottleneck, building a GTM plan for a known category.
- Danger: analysis paralysis. Experts keep demanding more data instead of deciding.

**3. Complex.** Cause and effect visible only in retrospect. The system is adaptive, and actions change the system itself. It may seem that careful analysis should work here. Yet, applying Complicated thinking to a Complex problem produces confident nonsense. **Probe, Sense, Respond.**
- _"We are entering the MSP market in a new region. What three pilot campaigns can we run in 90 days?"_
- Examples: launching a new product category, shifting org culture, entering an unfamiliar market.
- Danger: treating it as Complicated. You cannot analyze your way through a complex adaptive system.

**4. Chaotic.** No discernible cause and effect. Immediate action required. **Act, Sense, Respond.**
- _"Cloud infrastructure is down across three regions and customers are churning. What do we do right now?"_
- Examples: active security breach, PR crisis, system-wide outage.
- Danger: trying to plan. Any action beats no action. Move the situation out of chaos first.

**5. Confused (Disorder).** You do not know which domain you are in. People default to whichever domain matches their personal style.
- Break the problem into parts and classify each separately.
- _"Is our declining retention a Clear operational problem, a Complicated analytical one, or a Complex systemic issue?"_

**6. Match the approach to the domain.** Do not experiment where a checklist suffices. Do not analyze where only probing reveals truth. Do not apply best practices to situations that have never existed before.
- Ask explicitly: _"What domain is this problem actually in?"_
- Different domains require different leaders, decision processes, and failure tolerances.

**Practical notes:** Cynefin prevents category errors. Most organizations default to Complicated and apply it everywhere. A marketing team running 50-slide analyses on a Complex market entry produces beautiful decks and zero insight. The framework tells you *how* to decide, not *what* to decide. Pair with OODA for operational response and Design Thinking for Complex innovation work.
