# First Principles Thinking

Strip a problem to fundamental truths that cannot be reduced further, then rebuild your reasoning from there. The opposite of reasoning by analogy ("this is how everyone else does it"). It delivers the most when industry convention is wrong, too expensive, or too slow. Yet, it is the most cognitively expensive tool in this collection. Save it for decisions where stakes justify the effort.

**1. State the constraint you are taking for granted.** Most stuck thinking starts with an unexamined assumption dressed as fact. Surface it and write it down.
- _"Enterprise SaaS sales require a six-month cycle with four stakeholder meetings."_
- _"MSPs need a dedicated NOC team to offer 24/7 monitoring."_

**2. Ask where the "truth" actually comes from.** Is it a physical law? A regulation? A convention nobody revisited in a decade?
- **Physical or mathematical** → likely unbreakable.
- **Regulatory** → firm, sometimes jurisdiction-specific.
- **Historical or habitual** → very often breakable. Most business "rules" live here.

**3. Break the problem into raw components.** For a business problem: costs, inputs, people, time, and rules.
- Musk's rocket example: raw materials cost roughly 2% of the finished rocket's price. The other 98% is process, convention, and margin.
- MSP partner onboarding: irreducible steps are technical provisioning, commercial agreement, first-customer deployment. The portal tour, certification exam, three-week wait: process that accumulated over time.

**4. Identify which components are truly fixed and which are assumptions in disguise.** This is the core move.
- **Fixed:** physics, binding regulations, basic arithmetic, signed contractual obligations.
- **Not fixed:** supplier choices, process steps, org structures, pricing models, approval chains.

**5. Rebuild the solution from the ground up.** Ignore how it is "normally" done. Given only fixed constraints and raw components, what is the most direct path?
- You may land where convention already is. Fine. Now you understand _why_ and can defend it with substance.
- A SaaS company that realized its "mandatory" demo stage was a habit from on-prem sales replaced it with self-service trial and shortened the cycle by 40%.

**6. Pressure-test the rebuilt solution.** First-principles reasoning produces confident nonsense when a "fixed" constraint turns out not to be. Present your reasoning to a domain expert.
- _"Here is what I am assuming is fixed. Where am I wrong?"_
- You want the challenge before you commit resources.

**Practical notes:** First principles is a high-cost, high-reward tool, not a daily habit. Save it for when the conventional answer costs too much or caps growth. It also works best when you already have domain knowledge. In an unfamiliar field, you are more likely to rediscover someone else's mistakes than genuine fundamentals.
