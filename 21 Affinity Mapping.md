# Affinity Mapping

Affinity mapping (the KJ Method, after Jiro Kawakita) is a clustering technique for making sense of a large, unstructured pile of ideas or data. Write each item on a sticky note, spread them out, and let themes emerge by grouping similar notes. Use it after any divergent session when the real question becomes: what does all of this add up to?

**1. Collect the raw inputs.** One idea per note, short enough to scan at a glance. Digital equivalent: Miro, Mural, or FigJam.
- Sources: brainstorming output, customer interview quotes, NPS responses, retrospective items, campaign post-mortem notes.
- Aim for 30 to 150 notes. Fewer does not need clustering; more becomes unmanageable.
- _"Pull every piece of partner feedback from the last quarter. One quote per note, in the partner's own words."_

**2. Spread everything unsorted.** No predetermined structure. Resist the urge to organize as you go.
- Digital whiteboards: blank canvas, not a pre-made template.
- _"Dump all 80 notes onto the board. No categories, no headers, no structure yet."_

**3. Cluster in silence first.** Have the group cluster notes into natural groupings *without talking*. Multiple hands moving notes simultaneously.
- Silence prevents early debate and lets clustering emerge from the data itself, not from whoever argues loudest.
- Notes will move multiple times. That is normal.
- Typical duration: 15 to 30 minutes. The awkwardness fades around the five-minute mark.

**4. Name the clusters.** Once clustering stabilizes, start talking. Write a short header capturing each cluster's theme.
- _"Onboarding friction for mid-market accounts"_ beats _"things about onboarding."_
- Five to ten top-level clusters is the right granularity.
- _"If this cluster resists a clear name, does it need to be split?"_

**5. Find relationships between clusters.** Which clusters cause which? Which are in tension? Which are surprisingly related?
- Draw lines or arrows for causal or thematic relationships.
- Note clusters with very few items: weak signals of something important, or noise.
- _"Is 'pricing confusion' driving 'slow deal velocity', or are they independent?"_

**6. Extract the insights.** Translate the map into three to seven key takeaways for the next phase: ideation, prioritization, or a decision meeting.
- Capture surprises explicitly: _"We expected churn to cluster around product gaps, but it clusters around post-sale communication failures."_
- Photograph the wall or export the board. The artifact stays useful for weeks during planning.

**Practical notes:** The silent clustering phase is the part most teams skip, and the part that makes the method work. When teams talk while clustering, the loudest voice shapes the map and overrides the signal in the raw data. Yet, the discomfort passes quickly. Affinity mapping pairs naturally with any divergent method and works solo too: spread notes on a table and cluster. The physical movement helps the thinking in a way that staring at a spreadsheet does not.
