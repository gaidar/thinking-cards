# Design Thinking

Five-stage framework for human-centered problem-solving, formalized by IDEO and Stanford's d.school: **Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test**. The phases look linear but work iteratively. Insights from testing feed back into redefining the problem. The core commitment: design with users, not for them, and discover the right problem before solving it.

**1. Empathize.** Understand the people you are designing for, in their context. Observation and listening, not hypothesis-testing.
- Interview users. Shadow an MSP technician for a full shift.
- _"Walk me through the last time a backup restore failed at a client site."_
- Capture the gap between what people say they do and what they actually do. That gap is where real insights live.

**2. Define.** Turn empathy data into a focused problem statement: _"[User] needs a way to [need] because [surprising insight]."_
- _"MSP owners need a way to spot high-risk customers early because they lose more revenue to silent deterioration than to explicit churn."_
- A good statement is specific, actionable, and does not prescribe a solution. Formulate your core How Might We questions here.

**3. Ideate.** Generate a wide range of solutions. Go broad before narrowing.
- Use any divergent technique: brainstorming, Crazy 8s, SCAMPER, role-storming.
- Defer judgment. Aim for volume, not polish.
- End by clustering ideas and selecting two or three worth prototyping.

**4. Prototype.** Build fast, rough, cheap versions. Make the idea tangible enough to learn from, not to build the real thing.
- Fidelity should match the question. Early prototypes can be paper sketches or role-played conversations.
- If you are not slightly embarrassed by the prototype, you built too much.

**5. Test.** Put prototypes in front of real users. Do not defend. Do not explain. Observe.
- Set up tasks, not demos: _"Show me how you would check whether a client's backup completed overnight."_
- Treat negative feedback as gold. It is the cheapest lesson you will ever get.

**6. Iterate.** Testing feeds back into earlier phases. New insights refine the problem, trigger new ideas, new prototypes.
- Most real projects loop through phases three to five times before converging. Resist the urge to shortcut after the first round feels "good enough."

**Practical notes:** The biggest failure mode is skipping Empathize and Define to jump straight to building. Teams are eager to build, and framing feels slow. Yet, the framing phase is where projects are won or lost. For large initiatives, run multi-week efforts with dedicated research. For smaller problems, the Design Sprint compresses the whole cycle into five days.
