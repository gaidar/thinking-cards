# Mind Mapping

Radial, associative thinking on paper. Branches outward from a central idea rather than marching down a linear list. Works best for solo exploration, early-stage problem framing, and unpacking topics too fuzzy to outline directly.

**1. Put the central topic in the middle.** A single word or short phrase, circled. A small image next to it helps recall later.

- Keep the center specific. _"Partner onboarding friction"_ beats _"Partners."_
- Landscape orientation. You will need the horizontal space.

**2. Draw main branches for big categories.** First-level associations: themes, dimensions, or aspects of the central topic.

- Thick, curved lines encourage associative rather than hierarchical thinking.
- One word per branch. Five to seven main branches is the practical range.

**3. Branch outward with sub-ideas.** Each main branch sprouts thinner branches for supporting ideas. Go as deep as the thinking flows.

- Color-code each main branch and its children for pattern recognition.
- Write along the branch, not at the end of it.

**4. Add images, symbols, and connections.** Small sketches, arrows between branches, and highlight boxes add meaning that words alone miss.

- Dotted lines between branches on different sides of the map reveal cross-links, often the most valuable insights.
- Symbols speed up review: a warning sign for risks, a dollar sign for cost drivers.

**5. Let it get messy first.** The generative phase should be fast and associative, not precise. If you run out of room, tape another sheet on.

- Stop when new branches stop producing real content, not when the map looks full.

**6. Review and extract.** Step back. Look for clusters, gaps, and cross-links. Extract insights into whatever format you need next: a list, outline, or project plan.

- The map is a thinking artifact, not a deliverable.

**Practical notes:** Paper works better than digital for the first draft. Spatial flexibility matters more than you expect. Tools like XMind or Miro are useful for refining and sharing afterward. Yet, do not treat the map as a finished product. Use it to find structure, then move that structure into your actual deliverable.
