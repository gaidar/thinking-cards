# Design Sprint

A five-day compressed design thinking cycle, developed at Google Ventures by Jake Knapp (*Sprint*). Instead of arguing for months about what to build, a small cross-functional team goes from problem to tested prototype in one week. The fastest structured way to de-risk a big bet.

**1. Monday – Map.** Align on a long-term goal, map the user journey, interview internal experts, pick one target area for the week.
- _"In three years, every MSP running our platform resolves incidents proactively, not reactively."_
- Pick the riskiest or most important moment in the journey. Tackling the whole map produces shallow answers.

**2. Tuesday – Sketch.** Generate solutions individually. Review existing examples via lightning demos, then each person sketches a detailed solution.
- Silent and individual. Group brainstorms produce weaker ideas in sprint contexts.
- Everyone sketches, including non-designers. Rough is fine. Specific is required.

**3. Wednesday – Decide.** Critique sketches and choose what to prototype. Silent voting with dot stickers, structured speed critique, then **supervoting by the Decider** (the senior person with actual authority).
- If two ideas are both strong, prototype both as a "rumble": a comparative test on Friday.
- End the day with a storyboard: frame-by-frame plan of the prototype.

**4. Thursday – Prototype.** Build a realistic-looking facade. Not a real product. Keynote, clickable Figma, paper mockup. Whatever looks real enough for a fifteen-minute user test.
- Divide work: **Maker** builds, **Stitcher** assembles the flow, **Writer** produces content, **Interviewer** preps the Friday script.

**5. Friday – Test.** Five one-on-one user interviews. The whole team watches from another room. Patterns emerge by interview three.
- Note patterns, not individual reactions. Three of five users hitting the same wall is a real finding.

**6. Decide next steps.** Three outcomes: **ship it** (works, build for real), **iterate** (core is right, parts need rework), or **kill it** (test disproved the assumption). All three are wins. Killing an idea in a week instead of wasting a quarter is genuinely valuable.

**Practical notes:** Sprints need a committed week from five to seven people, a dedicated facilitator, and a clear Decider. Without those three, they stall. Best for high-stakes, high-uncertainty decisions; overkill for routine feature work. It may seem Friday tests could be skipped if time runs short. Yet, the tests are the entire point. Book users before Monday as a non-negotiable prerequisite.
