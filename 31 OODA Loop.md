# OODA Loop

**Observe, Orient, Decide, Act** was developed by USAF Colonel John Boyd to explain why American pilots won dogfights against technically superior aircraft. The pilot who cycled faster dictated the engagement. Since adopted in business strategy, cybersecurity incident response, and competitive operations, anywhere fast-moving situations reward speed over extended deliberation.

**1. Observe.** Gather raw information about what is happening right now. Not what you expected, not last week's report. Current, unfiltered data.
- Business: real-time customer signals, pipeline changes, competitor moves, operational metrics.
- Cybersecurity: live telemetry, alert patterns, network anomalies.
- Watch for absent signal. A customer who stops complaining is not necessarily happy.

**2. Orient.** Boyd's most important phase. Take raw observations and fit them into a mental model accounting for your history, assumptions, and the specific situation.
- _"What does this mean? What has changed since last time?"_
- Orientation is where biases and outdated models cause the most damage. A team that orients every competitive move as "they are copying us" will misread every situation.
- Explicitly challenge: _"If I am wrong, what would I be seeing that I am not?"_

**3. Decide.** Choose a course of action. Commit even when information is incomplete.
- Waiting for complete information is itself a decision, usually a bad one in fast-moving contexts.
- Treat the decision as a hypothesis, not a final verdict. This framing makes reversals easier.

**4. Act.** Execute. The action generates new observations feeding directly into the next loop.
- Keep actions small and reversible when possible. Large irreversible moves need a different process.
- Capture the effect immediately. It is the primary input to the next Observe.

**5. Loop faster than the opposition.** OODA's strategic core is relative tempo. If your loop runs faster, reality moves inside the opponent's decision cycle. By the time they have oriented, you have already changed the situation.
- Optimize the whole loop, not any single step. Fast Observe paired with slow Orient gains nothing.

**6. Disrupt the opponent's orientation.** Boyd's deepest insight: the real target is the opponent's Orient step. Confuse their mental model and their decisions become wrong regardless of speed.
- Unexpected pricing moves, surprise partnerships, rapid pivots all disrupt how competitors interpret your strategy.
- In security: feeding misleading information to an attacker degrades their reconnaissance and decision quality.

**Practical notes:** OODA is often reduced to "cycle fast and win." That is half the story. The Orient step (seeing the situation clearly) matters more than raw speed, because fast plus wrong loses to slow plus right. It may seem OODA applies universally. Yet, it is an operational framework, not a strategic planning tool. For multi-quarter planning, use a different frame. Pairs with Cynefin for situation classification and Lean/PDCA for structured improvement.
