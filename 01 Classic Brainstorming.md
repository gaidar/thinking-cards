# Classic Brainstorming

Separate idea generation from idea evaluation. It may seem obvious. Yet, most sessions fail because people critique ideas as they appear, killing the willingness to float bold ones. With disciplined facilitation, 30 minutes beats a week of email threads.

**1. Frame the prompt sharply.** A vague prompt produces vague ideas. State the problem as a specific question.

- _"How might we reduce SMB churn below 3% monthly?"_ beats _"Let's talk about churn."_
- Include constraints upfront: budget, timeline, what is off the table. One problem per session.

**2. Set the ground rules.** Osborn's four rules: defer judgment, encourage wild ideas, build on others' ideas, aim for quantity. Post them visibly.

- "No, but..." is banned. "Yes, and..." is encouraged.
- One unchecked dismissal shuts down the room for the rest of the hour.

**3. Generate in a timed burst.** Fifteen to twenty-five minutes is the productive window. The facilitator captures ideas visibly: whiteboard, sticky notes, or shared doc.

- If the group stalls, throw in a provocation: a new constraint, a wildcard persona, or an analogy from another industry.

**4. Build and combine.** Once the burst slows, invite remixes. Fuse two ideas, extend someone's thought, or invert an existing one. Stay in generation mode.

- This phase often produces the strongest output because people build on a visible inventory of raw material.

**5. Cluster and theme.** Group similar ideas into themes. This is a first pass at structure, not evaluation.

- Affinity mapping works well. Name each cluster in a few words for reference during convergence.

**6. Converge and select.** Now evaluate. Use dot voting, a decision matrix, or criteria like impact, effort, and strategic fit.

- Pick three to five ideas to take forward. Assign owners and define next steps before the meeting ends. Ideas without owners die within 48 hours.

**Practical notes:** Dominant voices crowd out quieter ones, and groups converge on the first decent idea. Counter this with five minutes of silent individual ideation before the group phase. The single most common failure is mixing divergent and convergent phases. Keep them cleanly separated.
