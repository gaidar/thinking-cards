# Pre-Mortem

A pre-mortem (Gary Klein) flips risk assessment by imagining the project has already failed and asking the team to explain why. The shift from future possibility to past certainty is psychologically powerful. People generate far more specific, honest failure modes when explaining a "fact" than when speculating about a risk.

**1. Set the stage.** Gather the project team. Lay out scope, timeline, and intended outcome so everyone operates from the same picture.
- Run near project start: enough shape to critique, enough time to change course. The week before launch is theater.
- Budget 60-90 minutes. Under an hour stays surface-level.

**2. Imagine the disaster.** Frame it vividly and with certainty.
- _"It is October 2026. The new partner portal launched six months ago. Partner adoption is at 8%. The sales team has stopped mentioning it."_
- Do not hedge. _"It could have gone badly"_ does not produce the same output as _"It went badly."_

**3. Individual writing: why did it fail?** Give everyone 5-10 minutes of silent writing. Each person independently generates as many failure causes as they can.
- Silent and individual is non-negotiable. Groups converge too quickly when people talk.
- Encourage items people would be uncomfortable saying out loud. This format makes that easier than any other method.

**4. Share and consolidate.** Go around the room. Each person reads their list; a facilitator captures items on a shared board. No debate, just capture.
- Group similar items as they come up. _"Under-staffed QA"_ and _"no dedicated testing phase"_ are the same concern.
- Watch for items said quietly or half-joked. Those are usually the most valuable.

**5. Cluster and prioritize.** Cluster items into themes (technical, organizational, market, execution, resource). Prioritize by likelihood and damage.
- A 2x2 matrix works well. High likelihood plus high damage gets immediate attention.
- Long-tail risks get noted but parked. You cannot mitigate everything.

**6. Turn risks into mitigations and owners.** For each high-priority failure mode, assign someone to design a mitigation: a plan change, a monitoring mechanism, or an explicit accepted risk.
- Unowned mitigations do not happen. Name a person and a date.
- Revisit at the project midpoint. The risks that surprised you are the most useful data for your next pre-mortem.

**Practical notes:** The key move is the switch from "what _could_ go wrong" to "what _did_ go wrong." Criticizing a plan feels like disloyalty; explaining a hypothetical failure does not. For major initiatives, run a pre-mortem at kickoff, a shorter one at midpoint, and a real post-mortem after delivery. The three-point cadence catches different risk classes at different phases.
