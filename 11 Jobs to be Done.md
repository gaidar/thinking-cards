# Jobs to be Done

JTBD reframes product thinking by asking what the customer is _hiring_ your product to accomplish. People do not buy a drill; they want a shelf on the wall. JTBD pushes you toward the real job and away from feature-level thinking. Most valuable when roadmap debates keep circling competitor features instead of customer outcomes.

**1. Identify the candidate job.** Describe who the customer is and what situation triggers them to reach for your product or a substitute.
- Format: _"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."_
- _"When an MSP signs a client with 200 endpoints, I want to deploy protection within one business day, so I can start billing immediately."_

**2. Interview for the real job, not the stated one.** Customers describe what they do. What they _need_ sits underneath. Trace the timeline of their last purchase or switch.
- _"Walk me through the last time you evaluated a backup solution."_
- _"What were you using before, and what made you switch?"_
- _"What workaround were you using before you found this product?"_

**3. Separate functional, emotional, and social jobs.** Every job has three dimensions. Ignoring two is why feature-complete products lose deals to simpler alternatives.
- **Functional:** _Deploy endpoint protection across 200 machines in under a day._
- **Emotional:** _Confident nothing will slip through. Not anxious at 2 AM._
- **Social:** _Perceived as the MSP that delivers fast and does not fumble onboarding._

**4. Map the job's full timeline.** The job starts before the customer touches your product and continues long after. Identify the **big hire** (deciding to act) and the **small hire** (picking a specific solution).
- What triggered the search? A breach? A lost client? A painful audit?
- What would make them fire your product and hire something else?

**5. Identify underserved outcomes.** For each job, list desired outcomes and rate how well existing solutions deliver. The gap between importance and satisfaction is your opportunity space.
- **High importance + low satisfaction** = focus here. Single-pane management across heterogeneous MSP environments – real gap.
- **Low importance + high satisfaction** = over-engineering. Cut scope.

**6. Design against the job, not the feature request.** A feature earns its place if it materially advances the job, not because it was requested or a competitor shipped it.
- Filter: _"Does this help the customer get the job done faster, cheaper, or with less risk?"_
- If a feature does not advance at least one dimension, it is roadmap noise.

**Practical notes:** JTBD delivers the most value at the strategic level: product direction, positioning, market segmentation. Yet, a well-written job statement is contagiously aligning: it changes how product, marketing, and sales talk about the offering for months. Do not confuse personas with jobs. The same IT director responding to a breach versus planning next year's budget hires very different products.
