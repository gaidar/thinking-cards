# Lean (PDCA)

**Plan, Do, Check, Act** is the core improvement loop of Lean and Toyota Production System thinking. Developed by Shewhart in the 1920s, popularized by Deming. Hypothesize a change, try it small, measure whether it worked, then standardize or adjust. The method's strength is explicit iteration: improvement from many small cycles, not one big plan executed on faith.

**1. Plan.** Identify a problem and design a specific experiment. This phase gets shortchanged most. Teams rush to "do" before knowing what they are testing.
- State the problem in measurable terms: _"Support ticket first-response averages 6.2 hours; the SLA target is 2."_
- Form a hypothesis: _"Routing tickets by product area instead of round-robin will drop first-response because specialists answer faster than generalists triaging unfamiliar products."_
- Define success metrics before the experiment starts. Keep scope small.

**2. Do.** Run the experiment on limited scale: a pilot team, a single product line, a two-week window.
- Capture data during the trial, not just at the end. Mid-cycle data reveals things final numbers obscure.
- Document what actually happened versus planned. Resist scope expansion mid-cycle.

**3. Check.** Analyze results against your hypothesis. Did the change produce the predicted effect? What happened that you did not expect?
- Distinguish between "did not work" and "worked but produced a side effect." Different implications.
- Talk to the people affected. Your support engineers will tell you things the dashboard cannot.

**4. Act.** Three options based on what you learned:
- **Standardize** if it worked: roll out broadly, update documented process, train people.
- **Adjust** if it partly worked: design the next cycle with a refined hypothesis.
- **Abandon** if it failed: document why, feed learning into the next attempt.

**5. Start the next cycle.** The output of Act becomes the input of the next Plan. In mature operations, PDCA cycles stack: daily at team level, weekly at function level, quarterly at org level.
- A cycle that outgrows its timeframe is miscalibrated. Split it.

**6. Build the rhythm.** PDCA's real value is cultural. Teams running dozens of small cycles per year consistently outperform teams attempting one large annual initiative.
- Treat the Check-plus-Act review as non-negotiable even when the quarter gets busy.
- Make learning visible: a shared board of "what we tried, what we learned" compounds into operational wisdom.

**Practical notes:** PDCA fails when teams do Plan-Do-Plan-Do without ever Checking. It may seem a long cycle covering a major overhaul would deliver more impact. Yet, a six-month cycle is not a cycle; it is a project wearing a Lean label. Two to four weeks is typical; for small experiments, a single day. Most powerful in operational contexts: support, deployment, onboarding.
