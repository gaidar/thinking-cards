# 5 Whys

A root-cause analysis technique from the Toyota Production System. State a problem, ask "why" five times, using each answer as the next question's subject. It sounds trivial. Yet, most teams stop too early or spiral into blame.

**1. State the problem as a specific, observable fact.** No judgments, no vague labels. Concrete starting points produce sharper chains.
- _"MQL-to-SAL conversion dropped from 38% to 21% this quarter"_ – workable.
- _"Our lead quality is bad"_ – too vague, you will chase ghosts.

**2. Ask the first why.** Answer with a verifiable cause, not an accusation.
- _"Because 40% of MQLs came from a single ungated webinar with no scoring criteria."_
- If you do not know, go find out. Guessing down the chain produces fiction that feels like analysis.

**3. Keep asking why, using each answer as the new subject.** Each "why" peels back one layer.
- _Why from one ungated webinar?_ → _"Campaign manager launched without scoring rules."_
- _Why no rules?_ → _"Scoring model never updated when we added webinar as a channel."_
- _Why not updated?_ → _"Ownership transferred during reorg; nobody picked it up."_
- _Why not?_ → _"No documented handoff process for marketing ops."_

**4. Stop when you reach something actionable.** Five is a guideline, not a law. You are done when the next "why" leaves your team's scope, or when you hit a systemic cause.
- Good stop: something you can assign to a person with a deadline.
- Bad stop: _"because people make mistakes"_ – that is a shrug, not a root cause.

**5. Watch for single-track drilling.** The method follows only one causal chain. Real problems usually have several.
- If the first "why" has multiple plausible answers, branch and drill each separately.
- A fishbone diagram complements well when you suspect parallel causes.

**6. Convert the root cause into a concrete action.** Diagnosis without a fix is an expensive conversation. Turn each root cause into a process change, checklist, or role clarification.
- Write an owner and a deadline. Unowned actions do not happen.
- Revisit in two to four weeks: did the fix hold?

**Practical notes:** The 5 Whys is routinely misused as a blame tool. Keep every question focused on the system, not the individual. When someone's behavior is the cause, ask why the system allowed that behavior to produce that outcome. That is where the real fix lives.
