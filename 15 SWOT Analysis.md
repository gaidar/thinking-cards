# SWOT Analysis

Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats. A four-quadrant framework for sizing up a strategic situation. It has survived six decades because the structure is dead simple and the quadrants cover what matters. Yet, its simplicity is also its trap. Most teams fill in the grid and call it done. The real value lives in crossing the quadrants to produce actual strategy.

**1. Define the subject precisely.** SWOT applies to a company, product line, team, campaign, or career decision. The subject determines what counts as internal versus external.
- _"Acronis Cyber Protect positioning in the European MSP segment"_ – specific enough.
- _"Acronis"_ – too broad to drive decisions.

**2. List Strengths (internal, positive).** Genuine, differentiated advantages, not table stakes. Every SaaS vendor has a website; that is not a strength.
- Validate with evidence. _"Strong brand"_ is a claim. _"Top 3 in four G2 categories, 800+ reviews averaging 4.5"_ is a strength you can build on.

**3. List Weaknesses (internal, negative).** Gaps, slow processes, technical debt, talent shortages, pricing complexity.
- Push the room to be honest. If weaknesses is shorter than strengths, the team is not trying hard enough.

**4. List Opportunities (external, positive).** Market shifts, competitor missteps, regulatory changes, underserved segments growing in size.
- Opportunities are external conditions, not internal ideas. _"Launch a new product"_ is an initiative. _"Mid-market MSPs are consolidating vendors and seeking single-pane solutions"_ is an opportunity.

**5. List Threats (external, negative).** New entrants, regulatory tightening, economic contraction, technology disruption, shifting expectations.
- Include slow-burn threats. Gradual commoditization typically destroys more value than a flashy new competitor.

**6. Cross the quadrants to generate strategy.** The grid is not the deliverable; the strategy is. Pair quadrants systematically:
- **S-O:** Use strengths to capture opportunities. Best offensive moves.
- **S-T:** Use strengths to defend against threats. Competitive moats.
- **W-O:** Fix weaknesses blocking real opportunities.
- **W-T:** Minimize weaknesses to avoid threats. Survival moves, unglamorous but essential.

**Practical notes:** The most common failure is stopping when the grid is full. A grid is not a strategy; the value lives in step six. However, SWOT works best with cross-functional participation: sales, product, ops, finance, channel. A single-perspective SWOT flatters the team producing it. Schedule 90 minutes and do not leave until step six is done.
